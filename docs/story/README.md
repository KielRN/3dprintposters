# Character Story Notes

This folder is the source of truth for the short stories attached to 3DPrintU
figurine characters. Each story should make the named person feel like the hero
of an original, relatable adventure that can be told in about two minutes.

The narrative mechanics are adapted from Kallaway's
[The Neuroscience of Addictive Storytelling](https://youtu.be/KyC8r-zitVE),
but these notes use prediction, surprise, and resolution as writing tools rather
than making public neuroscience claims.

## FIGURE Story Formula

| Step | Story job | Narrative mechanism |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **F - Familiar feeling** | Give the hero a feeling people recognize: fear, doubt, loneliness, pressure, or wanting to belong. | Relatable character |
| **I - Immediate stakes** | Give the hero one goal, one consequence, and a short clock. | Stakes |
| **G - Guessable problem** | Show a clear obstacle so the listener can predict what might happen. | Big question |
| **U - Unexpected truth** | Reveal that the obstacle was not what it seemed. Plant a small clue first. | Logical head fake |
| **R - Resolution earned** | Resolve the promised adventure and relationship tension through the hero's meaningful choice. | Earned ending |
| **E - Ending rehook** | After the payoff, open one new relationship or adventure question and end without answering it. | Rehook |

The required story order is:

> **Familiar feeling -> Immediate stakes -> Guessable problem -> Unexpected
> truth -> Earned ending -> Rehook**

The labels `Resolution earned` and `Ending rehook` preserve the FIGURE mnemonic
while keeping that narrative order exact.

`F + I` make the listener care. `G + U` create and break a prediction. `R` pays
off the story's main promise so the listener is not cheated. Only then does `E`
open a fresh prediction. The story stops before that final question is answered.

## ICP Story Direction: Relationship Plus Adventure

The current [business plan](<../../BUSINESS PLAN.md>) identifies the primary ICP
as sentimental gifters ages **28-55**, mostly women buying for a partner, parent,
or child. The secondary fandom/self-styler ICP is ages **18-35**. Therefore,
fourth-grade language is a readability rule, not a signal that the plots should
only be for children.

Every story needs two connected engines:

- **Adventure engine:** a quest, clock, danger, discovery, and visible choice.
- **Relationship engine:** a bond, an unspoken need, a wrong belief about the
  other person, and a meaningful change in that bond.

Useful relationship dynamics for the lead ICP include:

- Parent and child: protection versus independence, pride, trust, and legacy.
- Partners: hiding fear, sharing burdens, keeping promises, and showing up.
- Adult child and parent: role reversal, gratitude, memory, and inherited values.
- Siblings or close friends: rivalry, forgiveness, loyalty, and being truly seen.

The unexpected truth should change both the adventure and how the hero sees the
other person. The earned ending should resolve the immediate relationship
tension. The final rehook should expose a new secret, promise, danger, or piece
of shared history that matters to that relationship.

### Rehook Rule

The rehook is the final beat. It must do four jobs:

1. **Come after the earned ending.** The promised adventure and emotional change
   must already have a satisfying payoff.
2. **Open a new, specific prediction.** Use a concrete mystery, arrival, secret,
   warning, or choice the listener can picture and guess about.
3. **Grow from the story and relationship.** Reuse a clue, object, person, or
   promise that already matters.
4. **Remain unanswered.** The story ends on the open question. Do not explain it
   or soften it with a lesson afterward.

Use an ending shape such as:

> `[The adventure and relationship reach a payoff.] Then [a known clue changes
> meaning]. [End on the new unanswered question.]`

Useful terminal rehook patterns include:

- **Shared secret:** The companion recognizes a mark, song, name, or object and
  refuses or fails to explain it.
- **Unexpected arrival:** Someone connected to the relationship appears where
  they should not be.
- **Hidden cost:** The win activates a promise or danger the pair did not know
  existed.
- **Changed object:** A gift, badge, letter, seed, or weapon reveals a new
  message after the main problem is solved.
- **Impossible answer:** A signal receives a reply from a person or place thought
  lost.

Avoid vague endings such as "the adventure was only beginning." Give the
listener enough detail to form a real theory, but no answer.

## Reusable Draft Template

1. **Familiar feeling and relationship:** `{Name} cared about _____, but wished
   that person would _____.`
2. **Immediate adventure stakes:** `Before _____, they had to _____ or _____.`
3. **Guessable problem:** `_____ stood in the way. How could they succeed and
   still protect their bond?`
4. **Seeded clue:** `The hero or companion noticed _____, but misunderstood it.`
5. **Unexpected truth:** `The obstacle - or the companion's motive - was really
   _____.`
6. **Earned ending:** `The hero chose _____; the adventure succeeded, and the
   relationship changed because _____.`
7. **Ending rehook:** `After the payoff, _____ revealed _____. End before the
   hero learns why.`

## Writing Contract

Use these constraints for every customer-facing draft:

- Target **230-260 words** and roughly **1:45-2:05** when read aloud.
- Use short sentences with common, concrete words and active verbs. Dialogue is
  encouraged when it reveals the relationship.
- Write at or below about a **fourth-grade reading level**.
- Use one hero, one important relationship, one main goal, and no more than two
  other speaking characters.
- Put an adult-relatable relationship feeling inside an extraordinary adventure.
- Give the listener enough information to guess what might happen.
- Plant a visible clue before the unexpected truth.
- Use one logical surprise, not a random event.
- Let the hero's relationship choice solve the promised story. Do not rely on
  luck, an unexplained power, or someone else simply rescuing the hero.
- Give the listener the earned adventure and emotional payoff before rehooking.
- Put the unanswered rehook in the final beat, usually the last one to four
  short sentences.
- End immediately after the rehook. Do not add a moral or explanation afterward.
- Keep the story warm, safe, and family-friendly. Danger may create tension,
  but violence should not be the emotional payoff.

The shorthand is:

> **Extraordinary adventure. Relatable relationship. Earned win. Unanswered
> rehook.**

## Personalization And Voice

- Use `{Name}` as the reusable hero-name token.
- Use singular `they` so the story does not require a separate pronoun field.
- Keep the hero's name central, but vary sentence openings so the name does not
  feel mechanical.
- Treat the story as imaginative character lore, not as a claim that the named
  customer actually experienced the events.
- Favor care, courage, honesty, patience, teamwork, curiosity, and protection as
  sources of strength.
- Familiar heroes may guide the emotional lane, but never copy protected names,
  worlds, costumes, objects, catchphrases, powers, villains, or plots.

## Review Checklist

A draft is ready to compare when all answers are yes:

- Can a listener care about the hero in the first two sentences?
- Is there a relationship with a clear want, tension, or unspoken need?
- Are the goal, consequence, and clock clear?
- Can the listener form a reasonable guess?
- Is the clue visible before the reveal?
- Is the reveal surprising but logical?
- Does the hero earn both the adventure win and a relationship change?
- Is the promised story resolved before the rehook?
- Does the final rehook grow from the adventure or relationship?
- Does the rehook open a specific question and leave it unanswered?
- Does the story end immediately after the rehook?
- Does the story fit the length and reading-level targets?
- Is the story clearly original?
