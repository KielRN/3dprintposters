# Pilot: Chibi Heroic Fantasy Female

**Workflow style:** `chibi_female`  
**Status:** Pilot draft  
**Purpose:** Test the two-minute FIGURE formula across three ICP-relevant
relationship dynamics, each carried by a fantasy adventure and ending on an
unanswered rehook.

## Character Compass

This character is a small storybook guardian with a big sense of duty. Her
emotional lane may draw from the broad appeal of courageous fantasy heroines,
but her world, events, objects, powers, and language must remain original.

Shared traits across the pilot:

- Brave does not mean never feeling afraid.
- Care and attention are forms of strength.
- The hero notices what others misunderstand.
- The adventure win and relationship change are both earned through a choice.
- The main promise resolves before the final rehook.
- The final rehook grows from the relationship and remains unanswered.
- `{Name}` and singular `they` make each version reusable for a customer's hero.

## Option A: The Beast At The Bridge

**Relationship dynamic:** Parent and adult child - protection versus trust  
**Adventure lane:** Deliver a healing flower across a broken bridge  
**Length:** 258 words; about 1:47-2:04 aloud

> {Name} had trained for years to guard Willowmere, but people still called
> them the little guard. Their mother checked every strap on their armor and
> followed them to every gate. {Name} loved her, but wished she would trust them
> to lead.
>
> Before moonrise, they had to carry a healing flower across the old bridge. A
> sick child was waiting, and the flower would close when the moon rose. Their
> mother came along, even after {Name} said, "I can do this."
>
> At the bridge, a horned beast blocked the path. {Name} drew their sword. Their
> mother pointed to the beast's paw. It was pinned under a broken board. The
> beast was not waiting to fight. It was trapped.
>
> The flower was starting to close. {Name} could rush past or stop to help. They
> put down the sword and lifted the board. Their mother pulled the beast free.
>
> Then the bridge rope snapped. Their mother slipped toward the river. The beast
> caught her cape in its teeth while {Name} tied the rope around a post.
> Together, the three crossed before moonrise. The child got the flower, and
> Mother placed the guard badge in {Name}'s hand. "I did trust you," she said.
> "I came because even a leader should not stand alone."
>
> On the way home, the beast bowed. A silver mark shone under its horn - the
> same mark on Mother's old badge. Her smile vanished. "The Silver Guard has
> been gone for twenty years," she whispered.
>
> So why was one of its beasts waiting for them at the bridge?

FIGURE check:

- **F - Familiar feeling:** An adult child loves a protective parent but wants
  to be trusted as a capable leader.
- **I - Immediate stakes:** Deliver the flower before moonrise or a sick child
  will lose the cure.
- **G - Guessable problem:** A large beast blocks the only bridge while time runs
  out.
- **U - Unexpected truth:** The beast is trapped, not attacking, and the mother
  is the first person to look closely enough to see it.
- **R - Resolution earned:** The hero chooses compassion, then leads the rescue
  when the bridge breaks. Mother gives them the badge and explains that support
  is not the same as a lack of trust.
- **E - Ending rehook:** The beast carries the crest of Mother's lost guard. The
  story ends before Mother explains that shared history.

Editorial note: This is the strongest match for the lead sentimental-gifter
ICP. It can resonate in either direction: a parent recognizing a grown child or
an adult child honoring a protective parent.

## Option B: The Bell In The Dark

**Relationship dynamic:** Partners - hiding fear versus sharing the burden  
**Adventure lane:** Ring a warning bell before a storm reaches town  
**Length:** 249 words; about 1:43-2:00 aloud

> Rowan always knew when {Name} was worried. That bothered {Name}, because
> heroes were supposed to make other people feel safe. So when the old tower
> frightened them, {Name} hid it from their partner.
>
> A black storm was crossing the valley. Before it reached town, someone had to
> climb the tower and ring the warning bell. Rowan offered to come. {Name} said
> no. "Wait below. I'll be fast."
>
> Inside, the stairs turned above a deep, dark room. At the top, a shadow moved
> beside the bell rope. Was someone hiding there? {Name}'s hands shook. They
> almost called Rowan, but pride held their voice back.
>
> Lightning flashed. The shadow was only an owl tangled in the rope. {Name}
> freed it, but the rope dropped through a crack. The bell was high overhead,
> and the storm was almost there.
>
> From below, Rowan called, "Talk to me." This time, {Name} told the truth. Rowan
> found a loose chain on the wall and tossed it up. {Name} looped it around the
> bell. They pulled from both floors until the warning rang across town.
> Everyone reached shelter.
>
> When {Name} came down, Rowan did not laugh. "You don't have to hide fear from
> me," Rowan said. {Name} took their hand. "Next time, we climb together."
>
> Then the bell rang again.
>
> Neither of them had touched the chain. A second bell answered from the dark
> mountain, where no tower stood. Rowan turned pale. "My father used that call
> before he vanished."
>
> Who had just answered him?

FIGURE check:

- **F - Familiar feeling:** The hero hides fear because they believe their
  partner needs them to appear strong.
- **I - Immediate stakes:** Ring the bell before the storm reaches town.
- **G - Guessable problem:** A dark tower, moving shadow, and damaged bell rope
  make success look unlikely.
- **U - Unexpected truth:** The shadow is a trapped owl, and the deeper obstacle
  is the hero's refusal to ask Rowan for help.
- **R - Resolution earned:** The hero tells the truth. The partners use their
  separate positions to ring the bell together and keep the town safe.
- **E - Ending rehook:** An impossible bell answers with the private call of
  Rowan's missing father. The identity of the caller remains unknown.

Editorial note: This is the strongest partner-gift option. Its emotional payoff
is not "fear disappears"; it is "I can let the person I love see my fear."

## Option C: The Queen's Golden Seed

**Relationship dynamic:** Grandparent and grandchild - inherited duty and a
hidden family past  
**Adventure lane:** Return a stolen seed to the spring pool before sunrise  
**Length:** 246 words; about 1:42-1:58 aloud

> {Name}'s grandmother had cared for the queen's garden for fifty years. Now
> her hands shook when she held the tools. She gave {Name} the golden seed and
> said, "Tonight, our family's work becomes yours." {Name} felt proud - and
> afraid of letting her down.
>
> The seed had to reach the spring pool before sunrise. If it did not, no
> flowers would grow in the valley that year. Grandmother tied her old red cape
> around {Name}'s shoulders. "Remember," she said. "A true guard protects life
> before treasure."
>
> Near midnight, a tiny dragon stole the seed. Everyone said dragons loved gold,
> so {Name} chased it into the woods. But it did not fly toward the mountains.
> It followed a soft cry into a cave.
>
> Inside, a baby dragon shook from the cold. The older dragon had thought the
> glowing seed was food. {Name} took it back. Then they looked at the baby and
> remembered Grandmother's words.
>
> Sunrise was close, but {Name} wrapped the baby in the red cape. The older
> dragon breathed warm fire across the frozen road. Together, they reached the
> pool in time. The seed opened, and flowers covered the valley. Grandmother
> hugged {Name}. "You guarded exactly what mattered," she said.
>
> Then one flower turned toward Grandmother. A picture formed on its petals: a
> young woman in a red cape, standing beside that same dragon.
>
> Grandmother stepped back. "No one was ever meant to see that."
>
> What had she hidden from {Name} all these years?

FIGURE check:

- **F - Familiar feeling:** The hero wants to carry on a beloved grandparent's
  work and fears not being worthy of it.
- **I - Immediate stakes:** Reach the spring pool before sunrise or the valley
  will lose its flowers for a year.
- **G - Guessable problem:** A dragon steals the seed and seems to confirm what
  everyone believes about dragons.
- **U - Unexpected truth:** The dragon mistakes the glowing seed for food for a
  cold baby. Grandmother's strange rule now matters.
- **R - Resolution earned:** The hero protects the baby and the seed. That choice
  creates the dragon's help, saves the garden, and earns Grandmother's approval.
- **E - Ending rehook:** A flower shows Grandmother beside the same dragon when
  she was young. Her hidden past remains unexplained.

Editorial note: This is the strongest parent/grandparent keepsake option and the
best foundation for connected collectible lore.

## Pilot Recommendation

Use **Option A, "The Beast At The Bridge,"** as the first baseline. It best
matches the lead sentimental-gifter ICP while balancing adventure, kindness,
parent-child tension, a complete emotional payoff, and a relationship-based
rehook.

Keep **Option B** for partner and anniversary gifting. Keep **Option C** for
parent, grandparent, legacy, and memorial-adjacent gifting, or when later
stories should share a larger fantasy world.

Before publishing any option, read it aloud with a real customer name, verify
that the main promise feels complete before the rehook, and stop immediately
after the final unanswered question.
